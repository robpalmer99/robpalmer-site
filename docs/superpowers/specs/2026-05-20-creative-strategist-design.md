## Overview

A long-form pillar blog post covering the gap in our SEO strategy around the role of **creative strategist** — the job title that has emerged in the last 3-5 years to describe the AI-savvy, data-led version of what was historically called a copywriter or creative director.

The post is positioned as a **contrarian reframe**: the role is real and important, but most people calling themselves creative strategists today are missing one of three brains needed to actually do the job — direct response craft, AI/tooling fluency, or analytical rigour. Rob is positioned as the rare operator with all three (Triple Brain Marketing per `RobPalmer/offer_brief.md`).

Five sub-topics covered in a single pillar:
1. What is a creative strategist
2. What does a creative strategist do
3. Creative strategist vs copywriter
4. Creative strategist vs creative director
5. How to hire a creative strategist

Ends with a service pitch CTA. Per memory rule `feedback_post_format_default.md`, this is a keyword-targeted direct service pitch (not a listicle).

## Content Structure (target ~3,200-3,800 words)

### Opening hook (~200 words)
The job title that did not exist five years ago is now on every performance-marketing job description. Open with the gap: most "creative strategists" are repackaged copywriters or junior CDs who learned to spell "GPT." A real one is a different animal.

### `<KeyTakeaways>` (6 bullets)
- The creative strategist role is real but most people claiming the title are missing one of three brains
- Five core responsibilities, hypothesis-led ideation through performance iteration
- Why the role is not a replacement for copywriter or creative director — it is the merger of both plus a data layer
- The three brains problem: DR craft + AI/tooling fluency + analytical rigour
- How to hire one and what red flags to watch
- The ~10-minute read

### `<DefinitionBox term="Creative Strategist">`
AI-citable 3-sentence definition: someone who develops hypothesis-led creative concepts, briefs production, and uses performance data to iterate. Engineered for AI-driven media buying environments. Differs from copywriter (writes the asset) and creative director (owns visual/brand vision).

### Section 1: What Is a Creative Strategist? (~400 words)
Origin of the role — performance marketing teams discovered that volume of creative + speed of iteration mattered more than polished single assets. Someone had to own the concept-to-test-to-iterate loop. That someone got the title "creative strategist." Honest framing: the role is three older jobs in a trench coat.

### Section 2: What a Creative Strategist Actually Does (~600 words)
Five core responsibilities:
1. **Hypothesis-led ideation** — read the data, form a hypothesis about why winning concepts win, generate new angles to test.
2. **Brief writing** — translate hypotheses into briefs that designers, editors, and copywriters can execute.
3. **AI-assisted production at scale** — use tools (Midjourney, Claude, Runway, ElevenLabs) to compress production cycles from days to hours.
4. **Performance analysis** — read ad-account data, identify winning patterns, kill losers, scale survivors.
5. **Iteration** — close the loop: insight from data → next hypothesis → next test.

Reference the 60-20-20 rule from the copywriter post but reframe: creative strategists spend ~40% on data analysis, ~30% on hypothesis/ideation, ~20% on brief writing/production direction, ~10% on actual writing.

### Section 3: Creative Strategist vs Copywriter (~400 words)
Honest take: every great creative strategist is a copywriter underneath. The new job did not replace the old one — it expanded the scope from "write the asset" to "own the testing system that decides what to write next."

### Section 4: Creative Strategist vs Creative Director (~400 words)
Different muscle. CD owns visual/brand vision and a team. Creative strategist owns the testing system and the data loop. The CD job came from agency/brand worlds; the creative strategist job came from performance-marketing worlds. Both can be valuable. Most companies do not need both — they need to know which one they actually have a gap for.

`<ComparisonTable>` — 8 dimensions:
- Origin discipline
- Primary output
- Team structure
- Measurement
- Background
- Where they break down
- Compensation range
- When to hire each

### Section 5: The Three Brains Problem (~500 words)
This is the contrarian reframe and the positioning bridge.

Most people calling themselves creative strategists today are missing one of three brains:

- **Brain 1 — DR fundamentals.** They know how to spin up creative variations but cannot diagnose why a hook is failing. They have not internalized Schwartz on sophistication or Halbert on the AIDA structure. The result: high-volume variation generation that all dies on the same cold-traffic CPM math.
- **Brain 2 — AI/tooling fluency.** They know DR but they are still writing every variation by hand. Their throughput maxes at 5-10 concepts a week. They are about to get out-produced by a junior with a working Claude workflow.
- **Brain 3 — Analytical rigour.** They have craft and tools but they read ad-account data the way a horoscope reader reads tea leaves. Pattern matching without statistical literacy. Every "winner" is noise dressed up as insight.

The rare combination — Triple Brain — is what actually moves the metric. This section installs Belief 3 from the offer brief (a fourth category exists).

### Section 6: How to Hire a Creative Strategist (~500 words)
Bulleted checklist + red flags. What to ask in the interview. How to read their portfolio.

Questions to ask:
- Show me a creative test where you were wrong about the hypothesis. What did you learn?
- Walk me through how you read this last 30 days of ad-account data.
- What is your AI stack and what does it produce that you cannot produce alone?
- What is your weekly throughput in net-new concepts shipped to test?
- Name three direct response writers whose work you study.

Red flags:
- They talk about creative awards before they talk about CPA.
- Their portfolio is hero pieces, not test logs.
- They cannot name a hypothesis they were wrong about.
- AI/tooling fluency is asserted, not demonstrated.
- They have never lost a test.

### Section 7: What It Costs (~250 words)
- In-house salary: $80K-$180K depending on market and seniority
- Freelance day rate: $800-$2,500
- Retainer range: $5K-$25K/month
- Project: rare; this role is usually ongoing

### Section 8: The Pitch (~450 words)
Bridge from "how to hire one" to "the one with all three brains."

Triple Brain framing:
- 40+ years of direct response copywriting (Brain 1 covered)
- Claude Code with custom-built copywriting skills (Brain 2 covered)
- A 1,239-file Obsidian copywriting knowledge base (capacity multiplier for Brain 3)
- Plus: $523M+ in tracked results, Stefan Georgi/Justin Goff/Ben Palmer endorsements, Fortune 500 client history

Soft scarcity per memory rule `feedback_imply_dont_state_scarcity.md`: imply capacity through "currently accepting a limited number of engagements per quarter," not "last senior creative strategist taking clients."
